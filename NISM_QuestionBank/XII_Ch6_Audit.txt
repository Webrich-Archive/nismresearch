ISSUE #1 — Derivative Markets_19.xml (Expiry Day)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Question: "At any point in time, how many monthly equity derivative
contracts are available for trading?"
Correct Answer: "Three — near month, next month, and far month"
Explanation text: "expiring on the last Tuesday or Thursday of the
current month"

STATUS: PARTIALLY OUTDATED / NEEDS CLARIFICATION

The answer itself (three contracts) is correct per PDF Section 6.5.1.
However, the explanation mentions "last Tuesday or Thursday" which is
correct per the Dec 2025 PDF (which references SEBI Circular
SEBI/HO/MRD/TPD-1/P/CIR/2025/76 dated May 26, 2025). The PDF states:
"The monthly contracts expire on the last Tuesday or Last Thursday of
every calendar month depending on the choice made by the respective
stock exchanges."

The explanation also omits that quarterly (March, June, September,
December) and 8 semi-annual (June and December) long-maturity contracts
are also available. The PDF states these explicitly. While not critical
to the answer, the explanation should mention them for completeness.

FIX: Add a note in the explanation: "In addition to the three monthly
contracts, the exchange also specifies contracts for three quarterly
months (March, June, September, December) and 8 semi-annual months
(June and December). These are long maturity contracts."


ISSUE #2 — Derivative Markets_10.xml (Zero-Sum Game — Distractor Risk)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Question: "Derivatives are described as a 'zero-sum game' because:"
Correct Answer: "The gain of one party equals the loss of the other
party, with the net value of all positions being zero"
Distractor B: "No new asset or wealth is created in derivative trading"

STATUS: DISTRACTOR B IS ARGUABLY ALSO CORRECT

The PDF (Section 6.2.1) states: "The sum of the two positions is zero"
AND "There is no new asset or no new underlying created because of
derivative contracts on the underlying asset." Both statements appear
in the same paragraph as part of the zero-sum explanation. The PDF uses
the "no new asset created" concept as a direct consequence/corollary
of the zero-sum nature.

While the "correct" answer is the more precise definition, distractor B
is directly supported by the PDF text as a defining characteristic.
A test-taker who has read the PDF could reasonably choose B.

FIX: Rephrase distractor B to be clearly wrong, e.g.:
"No party makes any profit in derivative trading" or
"Both parties always break even in derivative trading"


ISSUE #3 — Derivative Markets_8.xml (European Options — Expiry Day)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Question: "In the Indian derivatives market, the options traded are:"
Correct Answer: "European options, which can be exercised only on the
expiry date"

STATUS: CORRECT BUT EXPLANATION NEEDS UPDATE

The explanation references contract codes "CE" and "PE" which is
accurate per the PDF. However, the PDF Section 6.5.4 states the expiry
date as "last Thursday of the month" in the general description, but
the updated SEBI circular (referenced in Section 6.5.1) now allows
"last Tuesday or Last Thursday." The explanation should acknowledge
this updated expiry convention for completeness.

FIX: Minor — add note that expiry is now "last Tuesday or Thursday
depending on exchange choice" per SEBI Circular May 2025.

SECTION 2: CONFIRMED FINE
========================================

Derivative Markets_1.xml — Definition of derivative
  Correct. PDF Section 6.1: "A derivative refers to a financial product
  whose value is derived from another." Perfectly aligned.

Derivative Markets_2.xml — Forward contract definition
  Correct. PDF Section 6.3.1: Forward is OTC, two parties, specific
  price, specific future date. All elements match.

Derivative Markets_3.xml — Forward vs futures distinction
  Correct. PDF Section 6.3.2: "Futures are exchange-traded forwards"
  with standardized terms and clearing corporation. Matches exactly.

Derivative Markets_4.xml — Call option definition
  Correct. PDF Section 6.3.3: "Option which gives buyer a right to buy
  the underlying asset, is called Call option." Right but not obligation.

Derivative Markets_5.xml — Option premium definition
  Correct. PDF Section 6.3.3 (Arvind/Salim example): "Arvind pays
  Salim Rs.100 as the upfront payment. This is called the option
  premium. This is also called as the price of the option."

Derivative Markets_6.xml — In-the-money call option
  Correct. PDF Section 6.3.3: When Nifty is at Rs. 10,200 and strike
  is Rs. 10,000, "Arvind's option is in-the-money." Market > Strike
  for calls. When Nifty at 9,800, option is "out-of-the-money."

Derivative Markets_7.xml — Option writer obligation
  Correct. PDF Section 6.3.3: "option writer has the obligation" and
  "when he decides to exercise, option writer would be legally bound
  to honour the contract."

Derivative Markets_9.xml — Interest rate swap
  Correct. PDF Section 6.3.4: Swap arrangement where one pays fixed,
  receives floating (T-bill plus spread). "Only the interest rate on
  this amount is exchanged" — principal is NOT exchanged.

Derivative Markets_11.xml — Physical settlement since Oct 2019
  Correct. PDF Section 6.2.2: "Since October 2019, SEBI has mandated
  physical settlement... for all stock derivatives." Index futures and
  options continue cash settlement. All facts match.

Derivative Markets_12.xml — OTC vs exchange-traded derivatives
  Correct. PDF Section 6.2.3: OTC = non-standard, counterparty risk,
  between institutions. Exchange-traded = standard, clearing corporation.
  "Forwards are OTC derivatives; futures are exchange-traded derivatives."

Derivative Markets_13.xml — Hedging definition
  Correct. PDF Section 6.7: "When an investor has an open position in
  the underlying, he can use the derivative markets to protect that
  position from the risks of future price movements."

Derivative Markets_14.xml — Speculation definition
  Correct. PDF Section 6.7: "A speculative trade in a derivative is
  not supported by an underlying position in cash, but simply implements
  a view on the future prices of the underlying, at a lower cost."

Derivative Markets_15.xml — Arbitrage definition
  Correct. PDF Section 6.2.4 and 6.7: Arbitrageurs exploit price
  differentials between markets. They "buy from the cheaper market
  and sell at the costlier market, and make riskless profits."

Derivative Markets_16.xml — Initial margin (2-day horizon)
  Correct. PDF Section 6.6.2: "Initial margin is collected using a
  formula that estimates the risk to an open position over a 2-day
  horizon." Exact match.

Derivative Markets_17.xml — MTM settlement on T+1
  Correct. PDF Section 6.5.3: "All derivative contracts are settled on
  T+1 basis." Settlement price = "weighted average price of the last
  30 minutes of trading." All facts match.

Derivative Markets_18.xml — Index futures launched June 2000
  Correct. PDF Section 6.4.1: "derivative contracts in the Indian stock
  exchanges began trading in June 2000, when index futures were
  introduced by the BSE and NSE."

Derivative Markets_20.xml — Open interest definition
  Correct. PDF Section 6.8.1: "Open interest is defined as the sum of
  all incomplete contracts in the market, represented as one-sided
  positions." Matches exactly.

Derivative Markets_21.xml — Put-Call Ratio > 1
  Correct. PDF Section 6.8.2: "PCR = Number of Put options outstanding
  / Number of Call options outstanding. If the PCR is greater than one,
  the number of puts is greater than the number of calls."

Derivative Markets_22.xml — Hedging a portfolio decline
  Correct. PDF Section 6.7 (Hedging): Portfolio manager can use
  derivative markets to protect portfolio from price movements.
  Selling futures or buying puts are standard hedging strategies
  implied by the PDF.

Derivative Markets_23.xml — Currency derivative for importers
  Correct. PDF Section 6.1.2 example (c): Indian importer worried about
  rupee depreciating can "enter into a contract that fixes the exchange
  rate of the rupee to the dollar on the future date."

Derivative Markets_24.xml — MTM calculation (Nifty futures)
  Correct. PDF Section 6.5.3 gives the EXACT same example: Purchase at
  Rs. 10,000, settlement at Rs. 9,900, lot size 50, loss = Rs. 100 x
  50 = Rs. 5,000 pay-in obligation. Numbers match perfectly.

Derivative Markets_25.xml — Call option net profit calculation
  Correct. The math is sound: Intrinsic value = 10,300 - 10,000 = 300.
  Net profit = 300 - 100 (premium) = Rs. 200. While this exact example
  is not in the PDF, it is a straightforward extension of the Arvind/
  Salim example in Section 6.3.3, and the logic is fully supported.

SECTION 3: CONCEPT GAPS
========================================
(Important PDF content NOT covered by any of the 25 questions)

GAP #1 — Clearing Member Types (HIGH PRIORITY)
  PDF Section 6.5.3 defines three types of clearing members:
  - Trading Member Clearing Member (TM-CM)
  - Professional Clearing Member (PCM) — banks/custodians, not traders
  - Self-Clearing Member (SCM) — settle only own trades
  No question tests this classification. This is a specific, testable
  fact with clear distinctions suitable for MCQ format.

GAP #2 — Trading Lot Minimum Value: Rs. 5 Lakhs (HIGH PRIORITY)
  PDF Section 6.5.1: "The trading lot for a futures contract is specified
  such that the value of the lot at the time of introduction is not less
  than Rs. 5 lakhs." Also repeated for options (Section 6.5.4).
  This specific threshold (Rs. 5 lakhs) is not tested in any question.
  It is a commonly tested numerical fact in NISM exams.

GAP #3 — Tick Size / Price Step: Re. 0.05 (MEDIUM PRIORITY)
  PDF Section 6.5.1: "The price step (also called the tick size) for
  futures contracts is Re.0.05." Includes a specific example
  (5610.50 -> next trade at 5610.55 or 5610.45, not 5610.52).
  No question covers this specific and testable fact.

GAP #4 — Market Timing for Equity Derivatives (MEDIUM PRIORITY)
  PDF Section 6.5.2: Normal market open 09:15 hrs, close 15:30 hrs,
  trade modification end time 16:00 hrs. No question tests these
  specific timings.

GAP #5 — Final Settlement vs Daily Settlement (MEDIUM PRIORITY)
  PDF Section 6.5.3 distinguishes: Daily settlement uses the weighted
  average of last 30 minutes; FINAL settlement uses "the closing price
  of the relevant underlying index or security in the cash market on
  the final trading day." This distinction is not tested. The PDF
  sample question #3 at the end of the chapter specifically tests the
  daily settlement price (weighted avg of last 30 min), suggesting
  NISM considers this important.

GAP #6 — Order Types in Derivatives Trading (LOW-MEDIUM PRIORITY)
  PDF Section 6.5.2 lists: Regular lot order, Stop loss order,
  Immediate or cancel order, Day order, Spread order. Also explains
  market vs limit orders. No question covers these.

GAP #7 — Weather Derivatives / Underlying Diversity (LOW PRIORITY)
  PDF Section 6.1.2 gives examples: weather derivatives (rainfall),
  interest rate swaps, currency derivatives, index derivatives —
  showing the diversity of underlyings. While Q23 covers the importer
  example, the broader concept of different underlying types beyond
  the standard equity/currency is not tested.

GAP #8 — Exposure Margin Specifics for Options (LOW PRIORITY)
  PDF Section 6.6.2: "Exposure margin is collected only on short option
  positions (sellers)." Also mentions premium margins and assignment
  margins for options. None of these option-specific margin details
  are tested.

GAP #9 — Position Limits and Monitoring (LOW PRIORITY)
  PDF Section 6.6.3: Positions monitored real-time, alerts sent,
  trading terminal inactivated if position limit is hit. Penalties for
  violations. No question tests position limit concepts.

GAP #10 — Benefits and Costs of Derivatives (LOW PRIORITY)
  PDF Section 6.7.1 lists benefits (hedging, liquidity enhancement,
  price discovery) and risks (OTC counterparty risk, liquidity crises).
  No question specifically tests the enumerated benefits/costs.

GAP #11 — Weekly Index Options Contract Availability (LOW PRIORITY)
  PDF Section 6.5.1: "seven weekly expiry contracts excluding the
  expiry week of monthly contract are made available for trading."
  This specific detail about weekly options is not covered.

GAP #12 — Base Price and MIBOR for Options (LOW PRIORITY)
  PDF Section 6.5.4: "The base value is computed as the theoretical
  price of the option, using MIBOR as the interest rate." The use of
  MIBOR specifically is a testable fact not covered.

GAP #13 — Corporate Action Adjustments (LOW PRIORITY)
  PDF Section 6.5.2: Describes how exchanges adjust derivative prices
  for bonus, rights, split, merger, dividends on cum/ex dates. This
  mechanism is not tested.

GAP #14 — STT on Derivative Transactions (LOW PRIORITY)
  PDF Section 6.4.2: "Securities transaction tax (STT) at the rate
  fixed by the government is also payable on derivative transactions."
  Transaction costs are "levied based on value of contract for futures
  and amount of premium for options." Not tested.

GAP #15 — SEBI Study: 9 out of 10 F&O Traders Lose Money
  PDF footnote 73 (Section 6.6): "A recent study by SEBI, published on
  January 25, 2023, revealed that 9 out of 10 individual traders in
  equity Futures and Options Segment, incurred net losses." While
  referenced in some explanations, no question directly tests this
  widely-cited statistic.
